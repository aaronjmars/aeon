# The Race to Understand Consciousness Before AI Makes It Urgent

Science has spent decades circling the "hard problem" of consciousness — why subjective experience exists at all. Now, a convergence of new tools, new theories, and the rapid advance of artificial intelligence is turning that philosophical puzzle into a practical emergency. In 2026, researchers are finally building the instruments to probe consciousness directly, even as they warn that the window for understanding it is closing fast.

## A New Window Into the Brain

At MIT, a team led by Daniel Freeman and philosopher Matthias Michel has laid out a roadmap for using transcranial focused ultrasound (tFUS) to study consciousness in ways previously impossible. Unlike older brain stimulation methods, tFUS sends acoustic waves through the skull to target brain regions just millimeters in size, reaching deep structures that transcranial magnetic stimulation cannot touch — all without surgery.

"This is a tool that's not just useful for medicine or even basic science, but could also help address the hard problem of consciousness," Freeman told MIT News. The key advantage is causal precision: most consciousness studies measure brain activity during subjective experiences, but correlation is not causation. By selectively stimulating or suppressing regions like the prefrontal cortex, tFUS experiments can determine whether a brain area actually generates conscious experience or merely reacts to it.

Freeman and Michel are already designing experiments targeting the visual cortex, aiming to establish cause-and-effect relationships in visual perception before moving to higher-order frontal areas. Their work could settle longstanding debates between "cognitivist" theories — which hold that consciousness requires complex information integration across the brain — and "non-cognitivist" views that locate it in specific local neural patterns.

## The Existential Risk of Not Knowing

The urgency is not purely academic. In a major review published in *Frontiers in Science*, a team led by Axel Cleeremans (Université Libre de Bruxelles), Anil Seth (University of Sussex), and Liad Mudrik (Tel Aviv University) issued a stark warning: progress in AI and neurotechnology is outpacing our scientific understanding of consciousness itself.

"Understanding consciousness is one of the most substantial challenges of 21st-century science — and it's now urgent due to advances in AI and other technologies," the authors write. Without reliable, evidence-based tests for detecting consciousness, society faces mounting ethical dilemmas. How should doctors treat unresponsive patients who may retain awareness? Do brain organoids grown in labs deserve moral consideration? And critically — could an AI system ever be conscious, and how would we know?

The researchers call for adversarial collaborations, where proponents of competing consciousness theories design experiments together, and for large-scale multi-laboratory studies to break out of theoretical silos. The stakes, they argue, extend to law, medicine, animal welfare, and the governance of AI systems.

## A Third Path: Biological Computationalism

Meanwhile, a new theoretical framework is challenging the assumptions underlying the AI consciousness debate entirely. In a paper published in *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, Borjan Milinkovic and colleagues propose "biological computationalism" — a middle ground between the view that consciousness is just software (run the right program and any hardware becomes conscious) and the view that it requires specific biological tissue.

Their argument rests on three properties that distinguish brain computation from digital computation. First, brains are hybrid systems: neurons spike in discrete events while being embedded in continuous chemical and electrical fields. Second, brain computation is "scale-inseparable" — you cannot cleanly separate the algorithm from the hardware, because processing at the ion-channel level is entangled with whole-brain dynamics. Third, metabolic energy constraints are not incidental overhead but fundamentally shape what the brain can represent and learn.

The implications for AI are sobering. If consciousness depends not on running the right algorithm but on a specific *kind* of computational organization — hybrid, multi-scale, metabolically grounded — then current digital architectures may be fundamentally incapable of producing it, no matter how large or sophisticated the models become. Synthetic consciousness, if achievable at all, might require entirely new physical substrates.

## The Clock Is Ticking

These three threads — new tools, new urgency, new theory — are converging at a moment when the question "is this system conscious?" is no longer hypothetical. As AI agents grow more autonomous and neurotechnology grows more powerful, the absence of a scientific answer to that question carries real consequences for how we build, regulate, and coexist with the minds we are creating.

The hard problem of consciousness remains unsolved. But for the first time, science has the instruments to seriously attack it — and the motivation to move quickly.
